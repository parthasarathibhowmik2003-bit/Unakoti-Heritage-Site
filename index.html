The Sacred Legend

The Curse of Lord Shiva

According to ancient Hindu scriptures, Lord Shiva once spent a night here en route to Kashi (Varanasi), accompanied by 99,99,999 gods and goddesses — one less than a crore (Unakoti).

He instructed his divine entourage to wake before sunrise and continue their journey. When dawn broke, only Shiva had risen. Enraged by their slumber, he cursed them all to turn into stone, leaving the site with one less than a crore deities frozen in time.

"Another legend speaks of Kallu Kumhar, a devoted sculptor who promised to carve one crore idols in a single night to earn passage to Mount Kailash with Shiva and Parvati. As the sun rose, he fell short by one — giving Unakoti its eternal name."

The Magnificent Sculptures

The images at Unakoti are classified into two categories: majestic rock-cut images on vertical surfaces and loose stone sculptures scattered across the hill.

30 FEET

Unakotiswara Kal Bhairava

The central and most conspicuous figure. This colossal rock-cut Shiva head stands 30 feet high including an embroidered head-dress of 10 feet. Features a vertical third eye, tribal-influenced headgear, and massive circular earrings.

West-facing Bas-relief
ROCK CUT

Gangadhara Shiva

Located at the right side of the approach-way, this image shows Shiva with Ganga flowing from his matted locks (jatabharas). Features rope-like hair design, tiara ornament, and two circular kundalas adorning the ears.

Depicting Ganga Descent
22 FEET

Colossal Ganesha Panel

A group of three Ganesha images of unusual characteristics. The seated Ganesha stands 7 meters (22 feet) high with a distended abdomen. The standing figures show Tantric Buddhist influence with attenuated waists and multiple arms.

Timeline

Journey Through Time

From the Pre-Manikya period to modern UNESCO recognition, trace the legacy of this sacred Shaivite pilgrimage site.

7th – 9th Century CE

Rock carvings created during Pre-Manikya rule. Patronized by the Deva Dynasty of Sribhumi in Samatata region. The site emerges as a major Shaivite Tirtha.

01
02

11th – 12th Century CE

Earliest literary references by A.K. Chaudhuri (1910-11) and Captain Williams (1914). Inscription on Chaturmukhalinga mentions pilgrim Sri Jayadeva. Pala empire influence.

1921 – 1922

Archaeological Survey of India publishes authoritative account in Annual Report. Site formally documented and recognized for its exceptional rock-art heritage value.

03
04

December 2022

Added to UNESCO World Heritage Site Tentative List. Government grants ₹12 crore for development. Modern conservation and tourism infrastructure initiated.

Living Heritage

Ashokastami Mela

Every year during the Chaitra month (April-May), thousands of pilgrims converge at Unakoti for the grand Ashokastami Mela. Devotees carry holy water from distant rivers to perform Abhishek on the Shiva sculptures.

On Shravan Mondays, the site transforms into a living Tirtha as young men and women undertake Kanwar Yatra, pouring sacred water on the ancient lingas and whispering wishes into the ears of Nandi.

Travel Guide

Plan Your Pilgrimage

How to Reach

By Air

Agartala Airport (178 km). Taxi/bus available to Kailashahar.

By Train

Kumarghat (26 km) or Dharmanagar (19.6 km) railway stations.

By Road

8 km from Kailashahar, 22 km from Dharmanagar. Buses and autos ply regularly.

Best Time to Visit

October - March Ideal
April (Ashokastami) Festival
July - September Monsoon

Budget 3-4 hours for the complete site visit. Wear comfortable shoes and carry water.

Stay & Facilities

Accommodation

Stay at Kailashahar or Dharmanagar (30-40 min drive). Limited guest houses near site.

Food

Local eateries available outside the main gate. Try famous Queen Pineapple of Tripura.

Entry

Security gate at entrance. ASI protected site. No guides currently available.
